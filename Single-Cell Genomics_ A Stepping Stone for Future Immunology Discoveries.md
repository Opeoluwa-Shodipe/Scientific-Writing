## Introduction
The immune system is the body's defense mechanism that detects and acts against cellular abnormalities and invading pathogens. This complex network comprises multiple components communicating dynamically at various stages and through different molecular means. Because disease prevention and progression depend largely on immune function, a deep understanding of the immune system is vital.  

**Immunology**, the study of the immune system, has long focused on characterizing and elucidating the roles of its diverse components. This deeper understanding enables the possibility of harnessing and manipulating the therapeutic potential of immunity for better disease management and intervention.

---

## Single-Cell Genomics in Immunology
Since the **19th century**, beginning with the pioneering efforts of *Ilya Mechnikov*, the classification and characterization of immune system components have relied heavily on **morphology** and **function**. With the advent of technologies such as **Fluorescence-Activated Cell Sorting (FACS)**, **Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)**, and **Mass Spectrometry**, researchers have been able to associate molecular markers, spatial organization, and tissue-specific relationships with various immune cell types.

Historically, it was believed that a differentiated cell acquires a fixed molecular identity that dictates its function. However, subsequent studies revealed that **cellular identity is highly versatile** and shaped by both tissue type and microenvironmental context.

---

## Power of Single-Cell Technologies
Recent advances in **single-cell genomics** have revolutionized immunology, providing insights that surpass the limitations of bulk analyses. For instance:

- Cell types once thought to be homogeneous, such as *hematopoietic progenitor populations*, have been shown to be functionally heterogeneous.  
- The discovery of **disease-associated microglia (DAM)** interacting with amyloid plaques in *Alzheimerâ€™s disease* underscores the power of single-cell analyses in uncovering hidden immune cell subsets and states.

To fully harness the capabilities of single-cell technologies, both **experimental** and **computational** pipelines must be carefully designed. Integration of these approaches allows single-cell genomics to move beyond static transcriptional snapshots toward **spatial** and **temporal** resolution of immune dynamics.
